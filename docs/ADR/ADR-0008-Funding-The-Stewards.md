# ADR-0008: Funding the Stewards

Status: Accepted, 2026-07-05. Opens and settles the funding question that ADR-0005 deferred. Builds on ADR-0005 and ADR-0002, and is grounded in the Week-2 volunteer-infrastructure research (research/Prior-Art, batch 2).

## Context

ADR-0005 retired monetization and, in its open questions, parked one thread for later: how pods sustain themselves in practice, following the Tor Project's nonprofit-and-institutional model. It also committed that if a funding question ever genuinely arose it would be opened fresh as its own decision rather than lingering as a hedge. The Week-2 research on whether volunteer infrastructure sustains has now forced that question, and this record opens it.

The research is unambiguous on one point. A Tor relay forwards cheap, non-rivalrous spare bandwidth the operator was not otherwise using; a DII node runs expensive, rivalrous GPU inference, where serving a peer means not using that card for yourself and paying real electricity. Every volunteer-infrastructure project that sustained did so on the cheap side of that line. SETI@home and BOINC ran on spare CPU cycles and still plateaued or declined even at near-zero donation cost. Folding@home is the one case that mobilized GPU donors at scale, and it did so only under acute crisis, then receded about 98 percent from its peak once urgency faded. Tor sustained for two decades, but through a funded nonprofit with a paid core team and a backbone of institutional operators, not through mass free volunteering, and still on cheap bandwidth.

The owner's read follows directly: some people may be willing to volunteer expensive compute freely, but a large, growing base of free GPU resources is unlikely. Relying on free contribution alone is therefore the assumption most likely to break the project, and organizing funding and sponsorship to sustain it is plausibly make-or-break.

## Decision

The network stays non-commercial, and funding the stewards is opened as a legitimate and probably necessary path, distinct from monetization.

Two commitments hold together. First, ADR-0005 stands without change to the network itself: no business model, no token, no tradable credit, no paid marketplace, and no paying of individual contributors. The network is free to use, access is never gated on contribution, and no one earns anything for running a node. Second, funding that flows into a non-commercial steward to sustain the mission is not monetization and is now in scope. Tor is the proof that the two coexist: it is free to use and non-commercial, yet funded by grants, donations, and sponsors that pay a core team and support operators. Money coming in to keep a mission alive is a different thing from money made from the network or paid to participants.

Three mechanisms are put in scope to develop, none yet chosen. A steward funded by grants and donations, most likely a nonprofit or a fiscal-sponsor arrangement, paying a small core team to maintain the software and network health; this is the direct answer to the labor cost that helped end SETI@home. Institutional operators as the backbone, meaning universities, labs, hackerspaces, libraries, and companies running high-capacity pods for their own people and sharing overflow; DII's existing pod examples already name these operators, and the research says to expect capacity to concentrate in a committed few by design rather than to spread across a flat volunteer base. And sponsor cost-offset, where sponsors underwrite hardware and electricity for the mission and its operators, on the model of the corporate capacity that helped carry Folding@home's surge.

The anti-model is excluded explicitly. Paying the individual contributor, or issuing a token or tradable credit, is out, because BOINC's Gridcoin experiment is direct evidence that it draws speculators rather than mission-aligned volunteers and does not grow the base. Cost-offset and sponsorship attach to the mission and its shared infrastructure, not to the volunteer as a wage.

The reason this is accepted now rather than deferred is that covering at least the bare-minimum expenses a node imposes on its operator, principally electricity and hardware wear, is judged essential to getting anyone on board at all, not an optional refinement. It is hard to see how a project could ask people to run expensive, rivalrous hardware for the mission while also leaving them out of pocket for it. This draws a clean line inside the anti-model. Offsetting real out-of-pocket cost so that contributing does not lose the operator money keeps participation mission-based, because no one is profiting; a payment beyond cost, a wage or a yield, is what remains excluded, and is what drew speculators to Gridcoin. Cost-offset restores the volunteer to break-even; it does not pay them to participate.

## Reconciliation with ADR-0005

ADR-0005 retired a "managed-federation funding path" as a commercial hedge. This ADR does not revive that. The thing retired there was a revenue path taken from the network, a business model wearing a funding costume. What this opens is the opposite direction of flow: funding into a non-commercial steward to cover costs the network itself will never charge for. The test that keeps the two consistent is direction and destination. Money out of the network, or into an individual's pocket for contributing beyond cost, stays forbidden. Money into a nonprofit steward, or into offsetting an operator's real costs to break-even, is what this ADR permits. Accordingly, the Architecture Overview line stating that no part of the architecture assumes or requires a funding path, and the relevant ADR-0005 wording, are updated alongside this acceptance to reflect that the steward now does assume a funding path while the network does not.

## Consequences

A steward entity becomes a near-term governance need rather than a someday item, which pulls forward the nonprofit-or-fiscal-sponsor question. Tor's funding history is both the model and the warning: it sustained on government-heavy funding and has spent years diversifying away from it, so DII should treat funding diversification as a first-class concern from the start rather than a later cleanup. Recruiting widens beyond end users: the demand probe in the kill criteria should now also probe for willing institutional operators and sponsors, since those, not a mass of individuals, are where the rivalrous cost gets carried. The batch-2 lesson that durability does not imply scale should set expectations toward a small committed core rather than mass growth, which fits the pod and community-scope design. And the one advantage none of the prior-art projects had, that a DII node also serves its own operator, partly offsets the rivalry and is worth leaning on, though it reduces rather than removes the funding need.

## Open questions

Which legal vehicle, an own nonprofit or a fiscal sponsor, and when. Whether cost-offset may flow to operators directly or only to shared infrastructure, and how to keep direct offset from becoming a de facto wage that recreates the Gridcoin dynamic. How to diversify funding sources from the outset to avoid Tor's single-source dependence. How this interacts with the optional reciprocity fairness knob left open in ADR-0005. These are the substance of turning this proposal into an accepted decision.
