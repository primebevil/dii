# Week 2, DePIN and Architecture Batches, Research Complete, 2026-07-05

Two more research batches finished the Week-2 prior-art work. Batch 3 covered the DePIN compute markets, the market DII rejected, and batch 4 covered the coordination and topology influences, read as architecture rather than as competitors. With these, all eighteen prior-art one-pagers across four batches are done, and Week 2 is essentially complete.

## Batch 3: the DePIN markets

io.net, Aethir, Render, and Akash, chosen to test the two threads the batch-1 synthesis left open: demand (Objection 2) and whether a scheduler is a defensible product (Objection 5). io.net is the cleanest confirmation of the demand objection, and it inverts the usual worry: supply is trivially abundant, 327,000 registered GPUs at about 2 percent real utilization, with client fees running near 2.6 percent of what it pays suppliers, the rest funded by token emissions. Supply was never the bottleneck; durable paying demand is. Where demand is real it attaches to enterprise datacenter GPUs, Aethir's roughly 128 million dollars of 2025 revenue and Akash's professional-supply pivot, or to a pre-existing latency-tolerant workload, Render's rendering. And it is driven by price competition with the hyperscalers, the one axis DII does not compete on. Akash is the direct Objection-5 test and confirms it: it won on supply and price, not on a scheduling layer that is thin Kubernetes-plus-auction.

The batch both warns and reassures. The warning is that DII's consumer substrate is the hard case and its demand must be non-price, sovereignty and access rather than cheap compute. The reassurance is that Render validates the latency-tolerant, node-local workload choice, and being non-commercial with no token sidesteps the emissions treadmill that collapsed io.net's token. The verification pass earned its keep again, correcting Aethir's token figures, Render's frame count and a misread statistic, and an io.net spike number.

## Batch 4: the architecture influences

BitTorrent, the Fediverse and Matrix, Ray, and the IETF decentralization work. This batch tied the whole research arc together, and it did so mostly in DII's favor. BitTorrent is the existence proof that trackerless DHT discovery and swarm resilience endure, and the Fediverse and Matrix prove that signed, DNS-like federation with no central index runs in production, so DII's intended topology is not speculative. Ray is the clean answer to Objection 5: capability-aware scheduling inside a trusted cluster is already commoditized, and Ray's own security documentation disclaims the untrusted, federated, sovereignty-constrained regime that is DII's entire reason to exist, so DII must be the trust layer Ray refuses to be rather than a better cluster scheduler. The IETF work is not a product but a frame: RFC 9518 as a usable definition of centralization, the re-centralization warnings as a checklist, and W3C DIDs and DNSSEC to align identity with rather than reinvent.

Two cross-cutting lessons landed hard. BitTorrent's tit-for-tat depends on non-rivalrous, cacheable content, so it does not solve DII's incentive problem, which is DII's alone. And the recurring failure mode of real federations is operator burnout and steward funding, the same conclusion batch 2 reached, and the strongest external validation yet for ADR-0008. The Matrix Foundation's repeated funding crises and Mastodon's founder stepping down from burnout are the concrete evidence that the architecture is the easy part and funding the steward is the hard part.

## Where Week 2 leaves the project

The four batches converge. The split-inference and distributed-training efforts stalled on physics or unproven economics, the pure-donation efforts on scale or retention, the market efforts on demand, and the durable decentralized architectures endure but hand DII two problems of its own, the rivalrous incentive and the funded steward. Both of those are already named, the second decided in ADR-0008. Nothing in the research refuted the surviving thesis from the teardown; it sharpened it. DII's bet is the narrow path through all of it: mission and community at pod scale, benefit ungated on the Tor model, a latency-tolerant workload that fits on one node, a non-price demand thesis of sovereignty and access, a proven federated topology, and a funded steward with cost-offset operators.
