# Prior-Art Research: Final Report

The capstone of the Week-2 research. Eighteen projects across four batches were written up as one-pagers, each stating what it does, whether it won or stalled and why, and how it compares to DII. The batches were then reviewed in discussion, which turned findings into design decisions. This report says what the research and the review concluded together. For the batch-1 deep dive see [Synthesis](Synthesis.md); for source confirmation see the [Validation-Checklist](Validation-Checklist.md); for the raw discussion record see [../Notes/Final-Report-Notes.md](../Notes/Final-Report-Notes.md).

## Purpose and method

The Charter defines success as understanding, and the teardown, [The Case Against DII](../The-Case-Against-DII.md), tried to kill the idea from first principles. This research met the teardown from the evidence side: rather than argue whether DII could work, it looked at what actually happened to the projects that tried adjacent things. Each one-pager was researched first, then checked by an independent verification pass that did not see the write-up, with every load-bearing figure mapped to a source. Four such passes ran, and they caught real errors before they landed, mostly in self-reported DePIN figures. After the research was complete, the four batches were talked through and the findings were pushed into decisions, which is where the sharpest conclusions came from.

## The four batches

Batch 1, decentralized LLM compute, is the substrate closest to DII: Petals, Parallax, FusionAI, Prime Intellect, Gensyn, and DisTrO. These split into two hard problems, sharing one model across many GPUs to serve it, and pooling many machines to train one, and DII does neither. Their complexity is the cost of the two things DII declines, splitting models and trusting or paying strangers, which confirms the teardown's physics objection and is why DII stays node-local. What DII takes is narrow: the verification family, TOPLOC and Verde, for the rare cross-boundary case, and the memory-and-latency wall as the reason to keep to models that fit on one node.

Batch 2, volunteer infrastructure, tests DII's participation bet: SETI@home, BOINC, Folding@home, and Tor. Mission-driven volunteer infrastructure can be durable, but every case that sustained did so on cheap, non-rivalrous donation. Folding@home mobilized rivalrous GPU donors only under crisis, then receded about 98 percent. DII's ask, expensive rivalrous GPU inference as an everyday baseline, is the variable none of the durable cases had to overcome, which produced ADR-0008.

Batch 3, the DePIN compute markets, tests the market DII rejected: io.net, Aethir, Render, and Akash. Supply is trivially abundant, io.net registered 327,000 GPUs at about 2 percent utilization, and paying demand is the scarce side, which cheap compute does not create. Where DePIN demand is real it attaches to enterprise datacenter hardware or a pre-existing latency-tolerant workload, and it is driven by price competition DII does not enter. Akash confirms the scheduler objection: it won on supply and price, not scheduling.

Batch 4, coordination and topology, reads as architecture: BitTorrent, the Fediverse and Matrix, Ray, and the IETF decentralization work. It is mostly confirmation. Trackerless DHT discovery and signed, DNS-like federation run in production and endure, so DII's topology is proven. Ray is the clean answer to the scheduler objection: capability-aware placement inside a trusted cluster is commoditized, and Ray's own security docs disclaim the untrusted, federated regime that is DII's reason to exist. The IETF work is a frame to align with, not a spec to implement.

## What the eighteen say together

The batches converge on one shape. The split-inference and training efforts stalled on physics or unproven economics. The pure-donation efforts stalled on scale or retention. The market efforts stalled on demand. The durable decentralized architectures endure, but hand DII two problems of its own: the rivalrous incentive, which BitTorrent's tit-for-tat cannot solve because inference is not non-rivalrous content, and the funded steward, which every real federation, from Matrix to Mastodon, shows is the recurring point of failure.

None of this refuted the surviving thesis. It sharpened it. The maximal vision, home hardware competing as a cost-driven compute layer, is contradicted on physics, demand, and trust-economics, now with eighteen cases of evidence behind the claim. The narrow version is not refuted and is the one the evidence leaves standing: a sovereignty-and-privacy-first router for latency-tolerant, node-local inference across a federation of trusted pods, motivated by mission rather than price, funded through a steward rather than a market.

## What the research produced: decisions and design conclusions

The research did not just test the project; it produced or confirmed most of the current decisions, and the review turned several findings into new ones.

Demand is the hardest open question, and the review sharpened it. Demand for DII is latent and trigger-dependent, not absent. Access is abundant and mostly affordable now, so the pain is low; it spikes when a blocker fires, price, gating, or a residency rule. The project's founding event, the export-control shutdown of Fable 5 and Mythos 5, is proof the trigger fires, so it is a precedent rather than a hypothesis. Dropping monetization clears a repellent but is necessary, not sufficient, since Petals had no token and still decayed. The demand probe is therefore two-part: the exposed-now minority whose alternative is nothing, and preparedness on the Tor model, existing before the moment arrives. Building pod-zero to learn is safe precisely because it is tiny and non-commercial, unlike io.net's token-funded glut. This refines the target definition in ADR-0007.

Why anyone contributes resolved into three parts, only one of them new to build. Mission is primary (ADR-0005). Cost-offset removes the money barrier (ADR-0008). Between them sits mutual benefit, the value a node gets simply by being in a pod: continuity when its own machine is busy or offline, and capability amplification, since a pod can run several models of varied use and each node reaches the union. Mutual benefit is emergent, unledgered, non-gameable, and free, and it is deliberately named apart from the Reciprocity Signal, the built priority credit, which it likely makes unnecessary. The honest limit is that a capable self-hoster's personal need is small, so they contribute mostly for mission; the real need lives with the person who cannot self-host. The rivalry worry, that sharing slows one's own machine, is answered by a load-bearing guarantee: local-first with idle-only, preemptible overflow the operator controls.

How it stays decentralized and serves everyone became ADR-0009. Re-centralization is resisted structurally, because sponsoring strangers costs real GPU time, so no pod can grow into an everyone-pod without collapsing; the same rivalrousness that makes contribution hard caps concentration by construction. The unaffiliated person is not served by one central pod but matched across many public-serving pods, the analog of Tor exit relays, through a decentralized directory. That gives a pod taxonomy, public-serving, private, and public-interest, and a funding rule: funding follows mission, proxied by whether a pod carries cost for people beyond its own members. Public-serving pods are the funding priority and a primary use of ADR-0008 money; public-interest pods like a research institute are funded case by case; private pods self-fund, because they already get their return as mutual benefit, and they cannot freeload because federation is bilateral. One principle keeps it honest: the steward is a legitimate center for governance and funding, but must never sit in the data path, tested by whether removing it would stop the network from serving. How that funding is raised is decided too, and it is voluntary: private beneficiaries such as a law firm or a corporate research team are invited, never required, to sponsor public access, and a paid tier or license for private use is reserved rather than adopted (ADR-0010), which keeps the network free while giving the steward a concrete, on-mission ask.

Why it cannot be absorbed is the answer to the teardown's Objection 5. DII is not a scheduler; scheduling mechanics are table stakes Ray and Kubernetes own inside a trusted cluster. The correction that matters is that sovereignty routing by itself is absorbable, a checkbox a hyperscaler can add, while the un-absorbable part is the substrate, a non-commercial federation of untrusted volunteer pods, because copying it means contradicting an incumbent's two assumptions, one trusted administrative domain and a paying-customer relationship. Ray's own security documentation, which assumes trusted code and a safe network, is the evidence that flipping this is a different architecture, not a toggle. So the moat is the substrate, and sovereignty routing is the visible product riding on it. The honest residual is that this closes defensibility but hands the weight to demand: uncopyable is worthless if unwanted, so this and the demand probe are linked.

## What DII borrows and avoids

Distilled, DII borrows a specific short list: DHT discovery and swarm resilience from BitTorrent, signed DNS-like federation from the Fediverse and Matrix, TOPLOC and Verde verification for rare cross-boundary trust, the latency-tolerant node-local workload shape validated by Render and forced by the physics, and Tor's steward-and-institutional-operator funding model. It avoids an equally specific list: splitting or training models, tokens and tradable credits and emissions, price competition with hyperscalers, reliance on free rivalrous donation or on crisis spikes, and any positioning as a better cluster scheduler.

## The open work

The research settled much and left a short list open, in rough order of impact. The demand probe is first and has the longest lead time: find people or organizations whose alternative is nothing, who would use DII specifically for sovereignty or privacy, and treat pod-zero as the instrument that learns this cheaply. Second, the load-bearing rivalry guarantee, idle-only, preemptible, operator-controlled overflow, must be built airtight, or contributors quit. Third, recruiting public-serving pods is the hardest supply task, the Tor exit-operator problem, and the main thing steward and sponsor funding must seed. Fourth, the funded steward is decided in principle but not in practice: the funding approach is now set (voluntary sponsorship, ADR-0010), but the legal vehicle, the donation and sponsorship channel, and the founder's near-term costs remain to be worked out. The identity-standards choice (W3C DIDs, DNSSEC) is deferred to Week 3, and the consumer directory and abuse-and-identity surface stay open under ADR-0002.

## Verdict

Week 2 set out to produce understanding cheaply, and it did, a week early. The evidence base now supports a clear, narrow, defensible version of the project and rules out the broad one, and the review turned that evidence into decisions: mission plus mutual benefit plus cost-offset for contribution, a public-and-private pod model with funding that follows mission, and a moat that is the volunteer-federation substrate rather than any feature. The prior art did not tell DII to stop. It told DII what to be, and the one thing still to prove is that someone needs it.
