# For Libraries and Civic Institutions

Status: v0.1 (2026-07-04). One-page pitch aimed at public and academic libraries, municipal digital-equity programs, and community foundations. Background in Outreach_Guide.md; hardware in Node_Requirements.md.

## The short version

DII is a volunteer, non-commercial project that keeps a dependable floor of open-weight AI running on hardware communities own, so everyday access to capable AI stays available even when centralized services are restricted or switched off. In June 2026 a leading AI model was disabled worldwide by government order for about two weeks before being restored, a plain demonstration that the AI people increasingly rely on is something they are permitted to use for now rather than something their community owns. DII lets an institution host a node that keeps a working floor of intelligence available locally, on the same civic-infrastructure logic libraries already apply to internet access and information.

## The precedent that makes this normal

Libraries have done almost exactly this before. Through the Library Freedom Project, public libraries began hosting Tor relays around 2015 to support privacy as public infrastructure, starting with the Kilton Library in New Hampshire. DII is the same civic move applied to access instead of privacy: where a library hosts a relay so privacy stays available to everyone, it can host a node so a dependable floor of capable AI stays available to everyone, including patrons who own no capable device of their own. This is digital equity in a familiar form, and it sits squarely inside the mission libraries already hold.

## The ask, from lightest to most committed

Lend your name and a little space: endorse the idea and let a node live on-site with power and a network drop. Host an anchor node the community runs: one always-on machine that serves the floor to a local pod and to patrons as consumers. Or, when budget and mission align, fund the purchase of that machine so the community owns its own node outright, the small-scale version of a community-owned data center. The model for that last step is community and municipal broadband, where towns pooled money to own shared infrastructure rather than rent it. One capable box (a 24 GB GPU machine or an Apple Silicon unit, quiet and low-power) is enough to anchor a pod; details are in Node_Requirements.md.

## Why it fits the mission

It extends equitable access to information into the age of AI, for patrons who cannot afford or reach commercial services. It keeps that access resilient, with no subscription and no upstream switch that a distant party can throw. And it is safe to stand behind: DII is non-commercial with no token, marketplace, or business model; it uses only open-weight models already legally published; it does not serve frontier or dangerous capability; and data stays inside the boundary you set, with any cross-boundary use opt-in and policy-controlled. A node that loses its connection keeps serving locally, so patrons keep access even during an outage.

## The honest caveats

This is an early project. The near-term goal is a working local node and a small pod that prove the floor is real, so an early library partner is a founding participant rather than a customer of a finished service. The models deliver dependable everyday capability, not the absolute frontier, which is the point. Some governance details, including how a pod decides whom it serves and fair-use limits, are still being worked out, and we would welcome a library's perspective in shaping them.

## Next step

A short conversation to find the comfort level that fits, whether that is an endorsement, hosting one node, or funding one. From there, a single node on-site is enough to show patrons a working, community-owned floor of AI.
