# Batch Review and Design Decisions, 2026-07-06

After the Week-2 prior-art research was complete, we talked the batches through one at a time, with me recapping each to trigger the owner's thinking. The discussions did what they were supposed to: they turned research findings into design decisions, and several are worth more than the reading that produced them. The running record is research/Notes/Final-Report-Notes.md; the durable decisions landed in the glossary, an ADR, the Overview, and the system diagram.

## Demand (batch 3)

The owner named the real fear plainly: he might be building something no one will use. The discussion split it into two. The commercial version, will anyone pay, is answered by not being commercial at all. The version that bites is will anyone use it even for free, and there the sharpening was that demand for DII is latent and trigger-dependent, not absent. Access is abundant and affordable now, so the pain is low; it spikes when a blocker fires, price, gating, a residency rule. The project's own founding event, the export-control shutdown of Fable 5 and Mythos 5, is proof the trigger fires, so it is a precedent, not a hypothetical. Removing monetization clears a repellent but is necessary, not sufficient, since Petals had no token and still decayed. The demand probe became two-part: the exposed-now minority whose alternative is nothing, and preparedness on the Tor model, existing before the moment arrives. Building pod-zero is safe as a way to learn precisely because it is tiny and non-commercial, unlike io.net's token-funded supply glut.

## Why anyone contributes (batch 4, item 1)

The contribution incentive resolved into three parts, only one of them new. Mission is primary. Cost-offset (ADR-0008) removes the money barrier. And in between sits mutual benefit, the value a node gets simply by being in a pod, continuity when its own machine is busy or offline, and capability amplification, since a pod can run several models of varied use and each node reaches the union. This is emergent, unledgered, non-gameable, and free, and it is different from the demoted Reciprocity Signal, which is a built priority credit. We named it "mutual benefit" deliberately to keep it apart from "reciprocity," and the consequence is that the reciprocity signal is probably unnecessary, because mutual benefit already answers "what do I get." The honest limit stayed: for a capable self-hoster the personal need is small, so they contribute mostly for mission; the real need lives with the person who cannot self-host. And the rivalry worry, that sharing slows my own machine, is answered by a load-bearing guarantee, local-first with idle-only, preemptible overflow the operator controls.

## Public and private pods, and who gets funded (batch 4, item 2)

This was the richest thread, and it produced ADR-0009. Re-centralization is resisted structurally, because sponsoring strangers costs real GPU time, so no pod can grow into an everyone-pod without collapsing; the same rivalrousness that makes contribution hard caps concentration by construction. The owner pushed hard on Jerry, a broke consumer with no pod, and rejected "come in through a community" as the primary answer, because it leaves the isolated person out and fails AI for All. The fix is the Tor exit-relay model: some pods choose to serve the public, and the unaffiliated are matched across many of them by a decentralized directory, never one central pod. That gave a pod taxonomy, public-serving, private, and public-interest, and a funding rule: funding follows mission, proxied by whether a pod carries cost for people beyond its own members. Public-serving pods are the funding priority and a primary use of ADR-0008 money; public-interest pods like a research institute are funded case by case; private pods self-fund, because they already get their return as mutual benefit, and they cannot freeload since federation is bilateral. The keeper principle: separate the steward, a legitimate center for governance and funding, from the operation, which must have no chokepoint, with the kill-the-steward test on the Tor model.

## Positioning (batch 4, item 4) and identity (item 3)

Item 3, whether to align pod identity with W3C DIDs or DNSSEC, was deferred to Week 3, since the prototype should tell us what identity needs to do. Item 4, the answer to Objection 5, held with one correction that matters. DII is not a scheduler; scheduling is table stakes that Ray and Kubernetes own inside a trusted cluster. But sovereignty routing by itself is absorbable, a checkbox a hyperscaler can add. The un-absorbable part is the substrate, a non-commercial federation of untrusted volunteer pods, because copying it means contradicting an incumbent's assumptions of one trusted domain and a paying customer. Ray's own security documentation is the evidence. So the moat is the substrate, and sovereignty routing rides on it. The honest residual is that this closes defensibility but hands the weight back to demand: uncopyable is worthless if unwanted.
